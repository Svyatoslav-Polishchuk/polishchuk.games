WarForged

Crafting is the journey.

A blacksmith shop management game that reimagines crafting mechanics through meaningful player choice and resource scarcity.

Role: Game Designer Duration: 6 months Platform: PC Team: Solo project

Game Walkthrough

Overview

Traditional crafting systems in games often feel repetitive and lack meaningful choice. Players typically follow optimal paths, reducing crafting to a mechanical grind rather than an engaging creative process.

WarForged introduces a dynamic crafting system where every decision matters. The "Forge Memory" system remembers how items were crafted, affecting their properties and creating long-term consequences for short-term decisions.

Visual Design

Forge Interface

The main crafting interface emphasizes the tactile nature of blacksmithing with intuitive controls and visual feedback.

Customer Interactions

Rich dialogue system that reveals customer stories and builds emotional investment in each commission.

Resource Management

Detailed material system showing quality, origin, and potential uses for strategic planning.

Forge Memory

Items display their crafting history, showing how player decisions create lasting consequences.

Core Mechanics

Forge Memory System

Every item remembers how it was made. Rush a sword and it might break in battle. Take time with a wedding ring and it becomes a family heirloom. This system creates long-term consequences for immediate decisions.

Dynamic Resource Scarcity

Materials aren't just numbers in an inventory. Each piece of metal has a story, and scarcity forces meaningful trade-offs between current needs and future opportunities.

Customer Story System

Each commission comes with a story. A farmer needs a plow before harvest season. A knight requires armor for an upcoming battle. These narratives drive urgency and emotional investment.

Design Process

01

Research & Ideation

Analyzed existing crafting systems in games like Minecraft, Skyrim, and Stardew Valley. Identified pain points: repetitive mechanics, lack of meaningful choice, and disconnection from narrative.

02

Prototyping

Created paper prototypes to test core mechanics. Focused on the Forge Memory system and resource scarcity. Iterated rapidly based on feedback and playtesting sessions.

03

Digital Implementation

Built a functional prototype focusing on the crafting interface and Forge Memory system. Conducted playtests to refine mechanics and balance difficulty.

04

Refinement

Refined UI/UX based on user feedback. Balanced progression systems and added narrative elements to enhance the customer story system.

Challenges & Solutions

Complexity vs. Accessibility

Problem: The Forge Memory system was initially too complex, overwhelming new players while not providing enough depth for experienced ones.

Solution: Implemented a progressive disclosure system. Basic memory traits are introduced first, with advanced interactions unlocked as players gain experience.

Balancing Scarcity

Problem: Resource scarcity was either too punishing (causing frustration) or too lenient (removing meaningful choice).

Solution: Created a dynamic scarcity system that adjusts based on player behavior. Added multiple resource acquisition paths to give players agency.

Emotional Investment

Problem: Players weren't forming emotional connections with customer stories, treating them as generic fetch quests.

Solution: Redesigned the customer system to show consequences of player choices. Added follow-up visits where customers share how items affected their lives.

Reflection

What I Learned

  • Progressive disclosure is crucial for complex systems - introducing complexity gradually allows players to appreciate depth without feeling overwhelmed
  • Emotional hooks matter more than mechanical optimization - connecting mechanics to stories created genuine player investment
  • Paper prototyping saves time - testing core mechanics on paper prevented weeks of development iteration
  • Player agency drives satisfaction - giving multiple paths to success increased enjoyment even when players chose suboptimal routes

What I'd Do Differently

  • Include diverse players in testing from the start - initial playtests skewed toward experienced gamers and missed casual player pain points
  • Consider technical constraints earlier - better communication about system complexity could have prevented late-stage scope cuts
  • Focus on accessibility from day one - accessibility features should be designed in, not added later
  • Document decisions better - keeping a design journal would have helped track why certain choices were made
